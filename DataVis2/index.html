Obesity in Australia: Global and Demographic Perspectives

Obesity can be assessed in various ways, with the most common method being the Body Mass Index (BMI) scale, which is calculated using a person's height and weight. BMI values help classify individuals as underweight, healthy weight, overweight, or obese. For adults, the World Health Organization (WHO) sets the following thresholds: a BMI between 25 and 30 indicates overweight while a BMI over 30 is categorized as obese. However, different BMI cut-off points are applied for specific groups, such as children and pregnant women.

One interesting trend is that the share of people who are overweight tends to be higher in richer countries across Europe, North America and Oceania. Roughly a third of adults in Australia were obese in 2016. In countries such as India and Nigeria, that share was far lower.

Share of Adults that are overweight or obese in Australia

In 2018, after adjusting for age, the Western New South Wales PHN area had the highest prevalence of overweight or obesity, with 4 in 5 adults (79%) living with overweight or obesity. The Northern Sydney area had the lowest prevalence, with just under half of adults living with overweight or obesity (47%).

How common is overweight and obesity?

In 2022, based on measured data from ABS 2022 NHS of adults aged 18 and over, around 60% of women and 71% of men were living with overweight or obesity overweight or obesity. One interestring pattern is that obesity is more common in older age groups. In 2022, 15% of men and 16% of women aged 18–24 years were living with obesity, compared with 41% of men and 37% of women aged 65–74. Furthermore, for most measures except men tend to have higher rates than women.

Health impacts of overweight and obesity

Overweight and obesity is the second leading risk factor contributing to ill health and death. It's linked to 30 diseases such as cancer, diabetes, dementia and chronic kidney disease. Based on measured data from AIHW 2021 overweight and obesity has contributed to around 16,400 deaths. This bubble chart displays the premature deaths attributed to obesity per 100,000 individuals.

Rise of Obesity

Obesity rates have been steadily rising worldwide and Australia is no exception. Over the past few decades, the country has witnessed an increase in obesity with around 25% of Australian adults classified as obese as of 2022.

Recognising the urgency of the situation, the Australia’s National Preventive Health Strategy (NPHS) aims to improve the health and wellbeing of all Australians at all stages of life. Some of the key strategies and initiatives include halting the rise and reverse the trend in the prevalence of obesity in adults by 2030 and to reduce overweight and obesity in children aged 2-17 years by at least 5% by 2030.
